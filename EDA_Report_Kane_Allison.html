Diabetes BMI, and Other Risk Assessment Approaches: An Exploratory Data Analysis

Introduction

This EDA examines different assessment approaches to determining patient diabetes risk. Body mass index (BMI) has long been considered a major factor in patient diabetes risk assessment, despite evidence revealing it's inaccuracy in determining true body fat percentage. Waist circumference has been proposed as a more accurate metric of body fat percentage, taking into account weight distribution. Patient nutrition quality is another critical component of diabetes risk, especially among those underdiagnosed. Further, other factors that could contribute to diabetes risk, including a patient's gender, race/ethnicity, or socioeconomic status, are important to consider.

Diabetes risk criteria are changing, with an increasing number of lean-BMI patients receiving diagnoses. Characterizations of diabetes being a disease inextricably linked with obesity and high BMI are not completely accurate and often lead to delayed diagnosis in patients with lean BMI. Including assessment of a patient's diet quality and social determinants of health (including gender, race/ethnicity and socioeconomic status) can reveal patient populations where diabetes risk is underreported.

In this EDA, there are three major hypotheses relevant to diabetes risk that were examined.

  1. Waist circumference will outperform BMI in assessing risk to patient metabolic health in the general population and in at risk/undiagnosed patient populations.
  2. Waist circumference will outperform BMI in assessing metabolic health across gender, race/ethnicity and socioeconomic background with some variation across populations.
  3. Analyses of diet will enhance metabolic health assessments in general, at risk and undiagnosed patient populations.

Data Explained

Data Source

These hypotheses were assessed using data from the The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) (August 2021 - August 2023) published by the Center for Disease Control. There are five data sources included in this analysis from NHANES, including demographic information (1), nutrient/diet information (2), results of fasting glucose (3) lab tests, body (4) measurements taken during medical assessment, and diabetes diagnosis status (5).

Sources: (1) DEMO_L.xpt https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/datapage.aspx?Component=Demographics&Cycle=2021-2023

(2) DR1TOT_L.xpt https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/datapage.aspx?Component=Dietary&Cycle=2021-2023

(3) GLU_L.xpt https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/datapage.aspx?Component=Laboratory&Cycle=2021-2023

(4) BMX_L.xpt https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/datapage.aspx?Component=Examination&Cycle=2021-2023

(5) DIQ_L.xpt https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/datapage.aspx?Component=Questionnaire&Cycle=2021-2023

Each file has a corresponding codebook at the provided link, with information about each variable in the dataset.

Data Cleaning

Many variables in these datasets follow a numeric code system, with a specific number corresponding to specific health criteria. These numeric value codes were updated using thhe corresponding codebooks on the NHANES wesbite. Some variables outside of the scope of the analysis were excluded from the final dataset. In nutrient data subsets, variables to examine ratio of macronutrients to overall caloric intake were added. These five individual datasets were merged together on the respondent unique identifier number 'SEQN', aligning respondent data across datasets. Some respondent data was removed from the final dataset for missing key critical to our analysis. Those missing results from fasting glucose tests were dropped, as assessing their metabolic health would not be possible. Further, those missing BMI and waist circumference metrics were also removed from the dataset. Missing income data was imputed with the median income for the overall dataset. Those missing nutritional information were not removed from the dataset, but would be excluded from nutrition and diet quality analyses. Variable names were changed to be more descriptive and easier to understand without needing access to the NHANES codebooks. This final dataset was saved as 'nhanes.csv'

Summary

Conclusions and Project Intent

Overall, this project examined effectiveness of diabetes risk assessment tools across general, at risk and undiagnosed patient populations from different genders, racial/ethnic backgrounds and socioeconomic statuses. BMI, waist circumference and diet quality were all examined factors contributing to diabetes risk. Generally, waist circumference showed stronger correlations with high fasting glucose than BMI, indicating it would be a better metric to assess diabetes risk than BMI alone. Diet quality and macronutrient consumption did not reveal any strong correlation with fasting glucose, indicating that examining macronutrient intake is not a critical component of diabetes risk assessment.

Difficulties

Overall, the project had some drawbacks and restrictions in its ability to determine efficacy of diabetes risk assessment tools. A portion of respondents did not fully complete the macronutrient intake section of the NHANES, limiting number of participants in the final dataset. Further, the macronutrient data relies on patient recall of food consumed in the 24 hours preceding NHANES survey administration. This might not be a reliable or accurate way to report and assess diet quality, due both to poor patient recall and a limited observation window.

Next Steps

In examining these same questions, a larger respondent sample would provide greater insight. In particular, expanding the size of the undiagnosed subpopulation would be critical for determining patient risk. Further, pulling nutrient information from another component of the NHANES survey that assesses diet quality over the period of a week or a month could reveal stronger insights into the relationship between diet quality and diabetes risk.
